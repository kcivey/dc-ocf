DC Ward 2 Council Contributions (July 31, 2019, Report)

Keith C. Ivey, August 20, 2019

I analyzed the DC Office of Campaign Finance (OCF) reports for Ward 2 council candidates for the period ending July 31 (including Patrick Kennedy's amended filing).

Since all five candidates are seeking to qualify for public campaign financing under the Fair Elections Act, contributions all come from individuals and are limited to $50 (except for the candidate and their family, who can give up to $2,500). Therefore most of this analysis focuses on the number of contributors rather the amount raised, and there's nothing about corporate contributions because the candidates are prohibited from taking them.

The contributor numbers are interesting because they give a clue about how much support a candidate has. There were 11 people who gave to more than 1 candidate, including 1 who gave to 3, 1 who gave to 4, and 1 who gave to all 5.

Map of DC Contributors

This map shows the locations of all DC contributors. You can display them as individual points color-coded by candidate, as clusters, or as a heat map. The colors in the clusters have nothing to do with candidates; they just represent the size of the cluster. Similarly, the colors in the heat map just represent the concentration of contributors in an area.

A few dozen contributors are excluded because they had bad addresses (PO boxes, typos, missing or wrong quadrant, and other problems). I fixed a fair number of them manually, since I got a little obsessive, but that number isn't too bad out of 1,455 DC addresses total.

Contribution Statistics

This table is the only part of the analysis that includes amounts of money raised. The first column after the candidate name is the total number of contributions made (which is higher than the number of contributors because some people gave more than once).

The next three columns are the number of individual contributors in total, the number who live in DC, and the number who live in Ward 2. There may be a small number of contributors who are double-counted because they gave twice under different variations of their name or address, though I did try to standardize addresses to avoid that. The next four columns are the amount of money given by those three groups of contributors, plus another column for the amount given by the candidate and family members.

Next comes a calculation of the total amount the candidate would eventually have once the base grant and matching funds (for what's been raised so far) from the Fair Elections Act are included. John Fanning has not been certified for the program yet, but I included Fair Elections funds for him because he seems to have enough DC contributors (I assume there's a minor problem like bad addresses that can be corrected). Daniel Hernandez is nowhere near qualifying yet.

The next three columns show the percentage of the money given by DC residents, by Ward 2 residents, and by the candidate and family members. The last two columns are the mean and median of the contributions (combining all contributions for each contributor).

Candidate and family contributions are defined for this analysis as those that are not matched and have a higher limit. Some candidates and family members gave $50 or less and are matched like any other contribution. Note that Jordan Grossman's total amount raised will likely drop by $7,666 that he will need to refund because OCF changed its interpretation of the law limiting candidate and family contributions. It looks like Kishan Putta has 3 contributors who gave $50 twice each, so he may have to refund $150 (or maybe that's a reporting error, but his total would still be $150 lower).

In each column, the lowest number is highlighted in yellow and the highest in blue; which one of those is good or bad depends on the column.
